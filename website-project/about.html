CHARACTERS

Guy Montag

Montag is the main character and a fireman who first depicts the dystopian society in which he lives as seen through the eyes of a worker loyal to it, then as seen through the eyes of a man in struggle over it, and last as seen through the eyes of someone determined to escape it. Montag lacks knowledge for the most of the novel and just trusts what he hears. Montag's transformation is inspired by Clarisse McClellan, despite the fact that they have only recently gotten to know each other.

Clarisse McClellan

Clarisse refers to herself as "seventeen and crazy," and her rapid-fire inquiries and statements reveal an inquisitive and open mind to the world around her. Clarisse's inquisitive mind intrigues Montag despite his reaction that she "think[s] too many things," especially when he contrasts her to his own numb, obedient wife. Montag is also intrigued by Clarisse's relatives. Clarisse's family converses into the night while sitting around a table with the lights on, as opposed to being addicted to wall-sized television screens.

Mildred Montag

The only significant character in the novel who lacks any possibility of addressing her internal difficulties is Mildred. Her attempt at suicide indicates that she is suffering greatly and that her obsession with television is a coping mechanism for avoiding facing her life. But she has buried her genuine emotions extremely deeply. Even her own failed suicide attempt seems to have escaped her notice. She is a terrifying character since the reader would anticipate getting to know the protagonist's wife extremely well, but instead finds her to be unfeeling, aloof, and unreadable. Given that she is Montag's wife, her betrayal of him is even more terrible than Beatty's.

Captain Beatty

Beatty is a contradictory, multifaceted character. He is a book burner who is well-versed in literature and who, at one point, undoubtedly cared deeply about books. Note that Beatty's entire address to Montag about the history of the firemen is oddly ambivalent and simultaneously laced with irony, sarcasm, passion, and sorrow. Beatty calls books dangerous weapons, but he brutally manipulates Montag using his own book on learning.
